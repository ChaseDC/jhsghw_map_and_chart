The Exodus DC's Jewish Community from the City Center (and their Eventual Return)

Washington's Jewish community suburbanized rapidly in the 1950s and did not return to the central areas of the city until the late 1990s. Beginning in the 1950s, synagogues and other institutions left the city's central commerical corridors for upper Northwest and Maryland. This chart depicts the Jewish community's dispersal from downtown Washington over time. Jewish institutions' opening year are plotted against their distance from the U.S. Capitol. As the chart makes clear, Jewish institutions only returned to the city center in the late 1990s.


The Jewish community's suburbization parallels the broader trends of DC's white community. Even before the suburbs' rise, in the 1920s and 1930s, neighborhoods of northern cities were segregated, due to a combination of whites' aversion toward living with African-Americans and biased institutions, like racially-restricted covenants. Until the legal foundations of DC's covenants were weakened in the 1940s, those contracts boxed-in DC's African-Americans into subneighborhoods. Jews were also sometimes excluded, particularly west of Rock Creek Park.

DC suffered a severe housing shortage during World War II: the capital's population boomed while construction material and manpower were diverted to the war effort. After the war, DC's white population fell precipitously. Whites took new federal highways to newly-built suburbs, buying first homes with GI Bill loans that disproportionately benefited whites.

By the time DC's schools desegregated in 1956, the process of DC's white suburbanization was well underway. Half of Washington's 81,000 Jews already lived outside the city. But desegregation catalyzed white flight; whites living in segregated Anacostia protested in the streets.


A 1956 survey captured DC's Jewish community at the height of white flight. The survey found large percentages of Jewish families planned to move within six-months, mostly to Montgomery County and Northwest DC, west of Rock Creek Park: 21% of families planned to leave Northeast; 25% planned to leave Southeast and Southwest. Even in the bastion of Jewish DC, Northwest Washington east of Rock Creek Park, 16% planned to move.
